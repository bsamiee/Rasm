# Collapse Cost Functional

[COST_IS_A_COMPARATOR_NOT_AN_ARITHMETIC]:
- The cost figure is never compared as a scalar — the route is the candidate that *dominates* under an `Order<Candidate>`, where one candidate dominates another iff its covered-facet set is a superset and its declaration count is no greater. Scalarizing `C × V × F` into a single number is the loss the comparator avoids: two candidates with equal products can carry disjoint facet sets, and the product hides which facets each covers. The ranking is partial-order-by-coverage refined to total by tier, never a number line.
- The covered-facet set is the comparator's primary key and is itself an owner: a `HashSet` of facet symbols whose membership the candidate's single declaration yields. `HashSet.isSubset(demanded, covered)` is the admissibility gate — a candidate is admissible iff it covers every demanded facet — and `HashSet.difference(demanded, covered)` is the exact inventory of facets the candidate forces hand-spelled. The route reads the difference, never a count.
- The comparator is built once and applied at every concept, the dogfood of `Match.type` applied to ranking: a single `Order<Candidate>` is the corpus-wide owner-chooser tail, and a per-concept hand-written `if (covers && cheaper)` ladder is the loose spelling it deletes. The comparator composes lexicographically, so adding a tie-break dimension is one `Order.combine`, never a re-written ladder.

```typescript
type Facet = 'value' | 'type' | 'encoded' | 'codec' | 'equal' | 'keys' | 'arbitrary' | 'equivalence' | 'pretty' | 'fallback'
interface Candidate {
    readonly tier: number
    readonly declarations: number
    readonly covers: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>
}
const admits = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>, c: Candidate): boolean => HashSet.isSubset(demanded, c.covers) // demanded ⊆ covers
const handSpelled = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>, c: Candidate): number =>
    HashSet.size(HashSet.difference(demanded, c.covers))
```

[THE_ORDER_IS_LEXICOGRAPHIC_COVERAGE_THEN_PARSIMONY]:
- The owner-ranking `Order<Candidate>` is exactly two `Order` instances combined under `Order.combine`, and the combine order is the law: coverage first (a candidate covering more demanded facets always outranks one covering fewer), parsimony second (among equal coverage, fewer declarations wins). `Order.combine(coverage, parsimony)` is lexicographic — the second key is consulted only on a coverage tie — so reversing the combine order yields the misroute that picks the smallest declaration regardless of whether it covers the demand.
- Each key is `Order.mapInput` projecting the candidate onto a comparable scalar: coverage is `Order.mapInput(Order.number, (c) => HashSet.size(HashSet.intersection(demanded, c.covers)))`, parsimony is `Order.mapInput(Order.reverse(Order.number), (c) => c.declarations)` — parsimony reverses because *fewer* declarations is *greater* rank. The reverse is the load-bearing detail: an un-reversed declaration order ranks the most verbose candidate highest, the exact inversion of the collapse law.
- The route is `Array.max(candidates, byCollapse)` over the admissible candidates — the maximum under the comparator, not a scalar minimum of a cost product. The comparator carries the whole decision, so the routing site is one `Array.max` call and the candidate vocabulary is the array; a new owner tier lands as one array element the comparator already ranks, never a new ladder arm.

```typescript
const byCollapse = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>): Order.Order<Candidate> =>
    Order.combine(
        Order.mapInput(Order.number, (c: Candidate) => HashSet.size(HashSet.intersection(demanded, c.covers))),
        Order.mapInput(Order.reverse(Order.number), (c: Candidate) => c.declarations),
    )
const route = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>, candidates: ReadonlyArray<Candidate>): Candidate =>
    Array.max(Array.filter(candidates, (c) => admits(demanded, c)) as Array.NonEmptyReadonlyArray<Candidate>, byCollapse(demanded))
```

[LATTICE_TRAVERSAL_IS_A_SORT_NOT_A_WALK]:
- The cross-product lattice is never walked rung by rung — it is the candidate array `Array.sort`-ed under the collapse comparator, and the route is the head. Sorting yields the whole promotion ladder as data: `Array.sort(candidates, byCollapse(demanded))` orders every owner tier from least-collapsing to most, so the chosen owner is the last element and the demotion target is the next-lower admissible element. The ladder is a derived projection of the comparator, never a hand-enumerated `as const` of tiers.
- The three dimensions `C`, `V`, `F` enter the comparator as three `Order` keys combined in fixed precedence — `Order.combineAll([byFacets, byCardinality, byChannels])` — because each higher dimension's owner subsumes the lower exactly as the bedrock fixes the precedence. The combine order *is* the precedence: facets choose the family, cardinality chooses tagged-vs-single inside it, channels choose variant-vs-flat inside that, and a comparator built with channels first reaches the variant owner on a concept with no structural identity — the misroute encoded as a wrong combine order.
- Monotonic lattice growth is the comparator staying *stable* under one inserted facet: when the demanded set gains one symbol, the comparator re-ranks, and a correctly-sized owner's rank is unchanged because it already covered the new facet — `Array.max` returns the same candidate. The proof of an anticipatory shape is comparator stability across the demand-set's growth: a re-sort that promotes the chosen owner is the under-sizing the next-feature diff exposes.

[DEMOTION_IS_THE_COMPARATOR_REVERSED]:
- Demotion is not a second algorithm — it is `Array.max` under `Order.reverse(byCollapse)` over the admissible set, the cost-minimization dual of the cost-maximization route. The promotion route maximizes coverage-then-parsimony; the demotion route, run when an owner's covered set strictly exceeds the demanded set, minimizes tier among candidates that *still* admit the demand. The same comparator, reversed, finds the lowest admissible rung — over-tiering and under-tiering are the two ends of one sort.
- The demotion trigger is read off the comparator's own keys: a dead derived capability is a facet in the candidate's `covers` that is absent from `demanded`, so `HashSet.difference(c.covers, demanded)` non-empty is the over-tier signal exactly as `HashSet.difference(demanded, c.covers)` non-empty is the under-tier signal. The two differences are the symmetric diagnostic — the first counts ceremony, the second counts hand-spelled artifacts — and the route minimizes both to zero only when the candidate's covered set *equals* the demanded set.
- Demotion is bounded by admissibility, never by tier alone: the reversed comparator is applied to the *filtered* candidate set (`admits` still holds), so it cannot demote across a facet the demand needs. The identity-regime facet is the floor of this filter — a structural-value candidate carrying `equal` cannot be demoted below the lowest tier that still covers `equal`, which is why the demotion is a definition-time correction (the filter is the same admissibility gate the promotion ran) and never strips a demanded facet to reach a smaller declaration count.

```typescript
const demote = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>, candidates: ReadonlyArray<Candidate>): Candidate =>
    Array.max(Array.filter(candidates, (c) => admits(demanded, c)) as Array.NonEmptyReadonlyArray<Candidate>, Order.reverse(byCollapse(demanded)))
const deadCapability = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>, c: Candidate): HashSet.HashSet<Facet> =>
    HashSet.difference(c.covers, demanded) // non-empty -> ceremony the reversed comparator strips
```

[FACET_SET_IS_A_MONOID_THE_OWNER_CONTRIBUTES_TO]:
- The covered-facet set unions over the lattice, so the demand accumulated by a composite concept is `HashSet`-union of its parts' demands and the candidate's coverage is fixed per tier — the demand grows, the coverage does not, and the comparator re-ranks on the growing demand. The demand-accumulation owner is a `Monoid` over `HashSet` whose `combine` is union and whose `empty` is the empty set, so a concept's total facet demand is `combineAll` over its field demands, never a hand-summed count.
- The candidate's coverage is a *constant* of its tier read from one declaration, so the comparator's coverage key is a join between a fixed coverage set and a growing demand set — `HashSet.intersection(demanded, covered)`. Choosing the owner by the absolute size of `covered` is the misread: a `Schema.Class` covering eight facets is not better than a `Brand.refined` covering two when the demand is exactly those two — the comparator scores the *intersection with the demand*, so an owner covering facets the concept never reads scores zero on them and the parsimony key then demotes it.
- The singleton-demand case collapses the comparator's coverage key to a constant — every admissible candidate intersects a one-element demand at exactly one — so the lexicographic `Order.combine` degenerates to its parsimony tail alone, and the route is decided purely by `c.declarations`. This is the mechanism behind the corner owner: the bare primitive wins not by a special rule but because the coverage key is provably uninformative when the demand is a singleton, leaving the parsimony key as the sole discriminant. A routing code that branches on the corner is the special-case the degenerate-comparator argument deletes — the same `Array.max` resolves it.

[ANNOTATION_FACETS_ARE_THE_BREAK_EVEN_DISCONTINUITY]:
- The cost functional is *not continuous* across the facet axis, and the discontinuity is exactly the annotation-derived facets — `equivalence`, `arbitrary`, `pretty`, `fallback`, JSON Schema. A generated owner covers each of these for *zero* marginal declaration because it derives them from its `ast` (`Schema.equivalence(schema)`, `Arbitrary.make(schema)` read the existing node), while a raw `as const` table or a bare `Schema.declare` covers them at a *fixed unit cost per facet* — each demands a hand-supplied function. The comparator's facet-coverage cost is therefore a step function: zero on the generated tier, one-per-facet on the raw tier.
- The discontinuity inverts the parsimony key at the break-even point and the inversion is silent in the declaration count: a raw table costs *one* declaration for pure keyed dispatch, beating a `Schema.Class`'s one-plus-`Self` for the no-derivation concept — but the moment one annotation facet is demanded, the table's cost jumps by one hand-written function while the class's cost stays flat, so the comparator's parsimony key flips at the first demanded annotation facet. Reading parsimony without the per-tier facet-cost step is the misroute that keeps the table after the first `Equivalence` demand appears.

```typescript
const annotationFacets = HashSet.make<Facet>('arbitrary', 'equivalence', 'pretty', 'fallback')
const facetCost = (c: Candidate, demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>): number =>
    c.tier >= 3 // generated owners: derive every annotation facet from `ast` at zero marginal declaration
        ? 0
        : HashSet.size(HashSet.intersection(demanded, annotationFacets)) // raw tier: one hand-written function per demanded annotation facet
const trueParsimony = (demanded: HashSet.HashSet<Facet>): Order.Order<Candidate> =>
    Order.mapInput(Order.reverse(Order.number), (c: Candidate) => c.declarations + facetCost(c, demanded))
```

[EACH_ANNOTATION_FACET_HAS_A_DISTINCT_STEP_SHAPE]:
- The three annotation facets are not one uniform step — each carries a distinct marginal cost because each is a distinct derivation hook on the generated owner. `equivalence` is the steepest discontinuity: a hand-supplied `Equivalence<A>` on a raw owner must thread the structural recursion the generated `ast` does free, and the `EquivalenceAnnotation<A, TypeParameters>` is `(...equivalences) => Equivalence<A>` — *compositional* over the type parameters, so a container's hand-written comparator must accept and thread its element comparators, a cost a flat hand-written `Equivalence<A>` silently severs.
- `arbitrary` is the second discontinuity and its step is invisible until a property test runs: a raw owner with no `arbitrary` annotation has no `Arbitrary.make` source at all, so the facet is not *expensive* to hand-spell — it is *absent*, and the concept simply cannot be property-tested without minting a `LazyArbitrary<A>` by hand. The comparator scores the raw tier as not covering `arbitrary` rather than covering it at a cost, so the demand for property-testability is a hard admissibility failure on the raw tier, not a parsimony penalty — `HashSet.isSubset` rejects the table outright.
- `fallback` is the discontinuity that re-routes most aggressively because its hand-spelled form is *recurring*, not one-time: `DecodingFallbackAnnotation<A>` is `(issue: ParseIssue) => Effect<A, ParseIssue>` attached once at the owner, versus an `Effect.orElse` repeated at every read site on the un-annotated owner. The comparator's parsimony key must count the fallback's hand-cost as proportional to *read sites*, not to one declaration, so a fallback-demanding concept's raw-tier cost grows with consumers while its generated-tier cost stays one annotation — the steepest re-route gradient in the functional.

[THE_DISCONTINUITY_RIDES_THE_THREE_SLOT_TUPLE]:
- The annotation-facet cost is per-*side*, not per-facet, because the generated owner carries three annotation slots a raw owner has none of: a concept demanding the same derived facet on *both* the decoded and the encoded representation pays *zero* marginal declarations on the generated owner — both sides ride the one class declaration's slot tuple — but *two* hand-written functions on a raw owner, so the discontinuity's step doubles the moment both sides are demanded. The cost functional reads side-count, not facet-count: a dual-representation `equivalence` demand is one step on the generated tier and two on the raw, the gradient that re-routes a concept whose decoded and encoded forms both need a derivation.
- `Class.annotations(record)` re-annotates in place but *sheds* the `new`/`make` constructor, so adding an annotation facet *after* declaration is not free in the way the construction-time slot tuple is: the late annotation costs the `value` facet (the constructor) it drops. The comparator therefore prices "annotation known at declaration" (slot tuple, all facets free, constructor retained) strictly below "annotation added later" (`.annotations`, constructor lost) — the timing of the annotation is a coordinate on the cost, and a late identifier or arbitrary that must keep the constructor re-routes to the slot tuple, never `.annotations`.
- The annotation hooks compose with the constraint chain at fixed altitude and the composition is what keeps the derivation free: `equivalence` attaches *after* the filter/brand chain because it derives from the refined `ast`, so `Schema.equivalence(refinedSchema)` yields a comparator that already respects the refinement at zero marginal cost. A hand-written comparator declared beside the chain re-derives the refinement and is the parallel artifact the comparator's facet-cost step prices out — the annotation's zero-cost coverage is conditional on its placement after the constraints.

[BOUNDED_OWNER_IS_THE_COMPARATOR_WITH_A_FLOOR_AND_CEILING]:
- The candidate vocabulary has a `minBound` (the bare primitive, covering one facet) and a `maxBound` (the variant-union owner, covering the full cross-product), so the comparator is a `Bounded<Candidate>` — `{ compare; maxBound; minBound }` — and the route is `Bounded.max(bounded).combineAll(candidates)`, the `Monoid` the `Bounded` instance yields reducing the admissible set to its supremum. The bounded form is the law made total: every concept's route lies between the floor and ceiling, and the demotion floor is `maxBound` under the reversed comparator, the promotion ceiling is `maxBound` under the forward one — the same bound read from both directions.
- The `Bounded` floor encodes the deletion law as the comparator's identity element: reducing an empty admissible set is impossible (admissibility filtered everything out), but reducing a set whose only member covers exactly the demand returns that member as both the max and the min — the floor and ceiling collapse to one candidate exactly when the demanded set equals one tier's coverage, which is the comparator's statement that the owner is correctly sized. A gap between the bounded max and min over the admissible set is the over-tier-or-under-tier signal: the route sits at the max, the demotion target at the min, and their distance is the surface the demotion law strips.
- The bound is monotone with the demand exactly as the lattice is monotone with the cross-product: a demand gaining one annotation facet lifts the `minBound` (the bare primitive no longer admits) without lifting the `maxBound` (the variant owner already covered it), narrowing the admissible interval upward — the proof that growth lands inside the existing ceiling owner and only ever *removes* floor candidates, never adds a new ceiling. A demand that lifts the `maxBound` above the variant-union owner is the signal a genuinely new owner family is needed, the only condition under which the candidate vocabulary itself grows.
