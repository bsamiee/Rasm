# Deep Research on .NET 10 and macOS Libraries for a Construction Materials, Structural, and IFC-Centric Monorepo

## Executive assessment

For the monorepo you described, there is not a single public .NET library that already gives you a **comprehensive, manufacturer-grade construction-materials corpus** spanning masonry bonds and coursing, steel shapes, joists, hangers, nuts, bolts, IFC semantics, section properties, and analysis in one package. The strongest current ecosystem is instead a **composable stack**: one family for **taxonomy and structural domain objects**, one for **IFC/openBIM**, one for **geometry and section properties**, one for **analysis/solvers**, and one for **analytics/validation**. That is the right architecture for a future layout/composition engine anyway, because it keeps your materials catalog, standards logic, geometry, and analysis separable and versionable. This is an inference from the package families currently available on NuGet and GitHub, which concentrate on IFC/openBIM, structural taxonomy, section properties, and analysis rather than rich product catalogs.

The most important conclusion is straightforward: **VividOrange is viable and current for .NET 10/macOS; Wosad is not**. VividOrange’s package family targets .NET 6.0, .NET Standard 2.0, and/or .NET Framework 4.8, and NuGet computes compatibility through `net10.0` and `net10.0-macos` for those libraries. By contrast, Wosad’s published packages target **.NET Framework 4.5**, were last updated in **2016**, and .NET Framework is a **Windows-only** technology.

If the goal is a serious monorepo foundation for materials, composition, IFC, and structural intelligence on macOS, the highest-confidence package stack is: **VividOrange as the structural domain/taxonomy kernel; Hypar and xBIM as openBIM/IFC layers; Ara3D as a modern IFC/analytics bridge; UnitsNet, MathNet, CSparse, NetTopologySuite, and Clipper2 as numerical/geometry primitives; BriefFiniteElementNet as a managed FEA layer; and IDS/bSDD-related tooling for validation and schema-driven metadata**.

## Compatibility baseline

Your constraint set is internally consistent with modern .NET. Microsoft’s current support policy lists **.NET 10** as an active **LTS** release, and the official download page offers **SDK and runtime builds for macOS x64 and Arm64**. Microsoft also states that **.NET 5 and later continue to support .NET Standard 2.1 and earlier**, which is the key reason many `netstandard2.0` libraries remain usable in `net10.0`.

That means your practical screening rule should be this: if a package targets **.NET Standard 2.0** or **net6.0+**, it is usually a reasonable candidate for a `.NET 10` monorepo on macOS; if it targets **.NET Framework only**, it is effectively disqualified unless you are willing to fork and port. That is precisely why VividOrange, Hypar, Ara3D, BriefFiniteElementNet, Math.NET, NetTopologySuite, and most of the xBIM non-native packages remain credible, while Wosad and several older BHoM or Windows geometry components do not.

## What Wosad and VividOrange actually give you

### Wosad

Wosad’s public repository still shows an ambitious structural domain surface, including `Wosad.Analysis`, `Wosad.ColdFormedSteel`, `Wosad.Common`, `Wosad.Concrete`, `Wosad.Design`, `Wosad.Loads`, `Wosad.Masonry`, `Wosad.Steel`, and `Wosad.Wood`. On NuGet, the clearly surfaced published family around `Wosad.Common` includes at least `Wosad.Steel`, `Wosad.Concrete`, `Wosad.Analysis`, `Wosad.Wood`, and `Wosad.Loads`.

The problem is not relevance; it is platform and age. `Wosad.Common` targets **.NET Framework 4.5**, depends on older libraries such as `Clipper` and `Newtonsoft.Json`, and its latest NuGet update was in **September 2016**. Microsoft’s own documentation states that .NET Framework is **Windows-only**. For a fresh `.NET 10` monorepo on macOS, Wosad should therefore be treated as **reference code or a potential porting source**, not as a first-class runtime dependency.

My recommendation is to **catalog Wosad’s concepts**, especially its structural-material breakdown and its masonry/steel/wood intent, but **do not make it a hard dependency** in your primary cross-platform stack. If you care deeply about Wosad’s masonry or code-check logic, the realistic path is a selective fork-and-port into your own `net10` projects.

### VividOrange

VividOrange is materially different. The NuGet owner profile shows **31 packages** with active updates across **late 2025 and early 2026**, and the visible package set is unusually well aligned with the monorepo you want: it covers **geometry, materials, profiles, sections, loads, cases, stages, standards, serialization, uncertainties, and force-moment interaction**. These packages are generally published for **.NET 6.0**, **.NET Standard 2.0**, and/or **.NET Framework 4.8**, with NuGet computing compatibility through **.NET 10** and **macOS**.

The visible VividOrange package inventory is already rich enough to justify using it as your **domain kernel**. The package family currently includes: `VividOrange.ICartesianBase`, `VividOrange.IGeometry`, `VividOrange.Geometry`, `VividOrange.Geometry.Serialization`, `VividOrange.ISerialization`, `VividOrange.Serialization`, `VividOrange.IStandards`, `VividOrange.Standards`, `VividOrange.IProfiles`, `VividOrange.Profiles`, `VividOrange.Profiles.Perimeter`, `VividOrange.Profiles.Catalogue`, `VividOrange.IMaterials`, `VividOrange.Materials`, `VividOrange.ISections`, `VividOrange.Sections`, `VividOrange.Sections.ISectionProperties`, `VividOrange.Sections.SectionProperties`, `VividOrange.ILoads`, `VividOrange.Loads`, `VividOrange.ICases`, `VividOrange.Cases`, `VividOrange.IStages`, `VividOrange.Stages`, `VividOrange.IUncertainties`, `VividOrange.Uncertainties`, `VividOrange.Uncertainties.Quantities`, `VividOrange.IForceMomentInteraction`, `VividOrange.ForceMomentInteraction`, `VividOrange.InteractionDiagram`, and `VividOrange.Countries`.

That inventory matters because it maps very cleanly onto your monorepo’s needs. `VividOrange.Materials`, `Profiles`, `Profiles.Catalogue`, `Sections`, `Sections.SectionProperties`, `Loads`, `Cases`, `Stages`, and `Standards` are exactly the kind of modular boundaries you want if a later composition engine is going to reason about “what something is,” “what rules apply,” “what section it uses,” and “how it should be analyzed.” The package descriptions are explicit that these libraries are intended for **structural engineering design, analysis, specifications, and standards**.

The GitHub side also suggests that the VividOrange ecosystem is part of a broader, still-moving structural-engineering toolkit lineage now visible in `MagmaWorks` repositories such as `Taxonomy`, `SectionProperties`, and `SCaFFOLD`. That is a positive sign for future growth, even if you should still evaluate maintainer velocity before committing to it as a hard external dependency.

## Best-fit external package families

### OpenBIM, IFC, IDS, and schema-oriented libraries

**Hypar** is one of the strongest current candidates if you want code-first building generation plus IFC interoperability. `Hypar.Elements` describes itself as **“a building elements library for AEC”** and targets **.NET Standard 2.0**, while `Hypar.IFC4` is an **IFC library for .NET** targeting **.NET 6.0** and `Hypar.Elements.Serialization.IFC` adds IFC serialization utilities for `Hypar.Elements`. The Hypar repository itself describes Elements as a **cross-platform library for creating building elements**.

**xBIM** remains the most recognizable .NET IFC toolkit family, but you need to use it selectively on macOS. The high-value pieces are `Xbim.Ifc`, `Xbim.Common`, `Xbim.IO.MemoryModel`, `Xbim.Properties`, `Xbim.IDS.Validator.Core`, `Xbim.IDS.Validator.Extensions.COBie`, `Xbim.CobieExpress`, `Xbim.CobieExpress.Exchanger`, `Xbim.Cobie`, `Xbim.PropertySets`, and `Xbim.WexBlazor`. These give you IFC semantic access, in-memory models, property-set metadata, IDS validation, COBie conversion, and browser/Blazor visualization. By contrast, `Xbim.IO.Esent` is explicitly **Windows only**, and `Xbim.Geometry` is also **Windows only** because of its native Open Cascade dependency.

For **specification validation**, two families stand out. `ids-lib` is a reusable library for automating and auditing **buildingSMART IDS files**, and the official `buildingSMART/IDS-Audit-tool` repository states that it contains both a **CLI tool** and a **reusable library** implemented in Microsoft .NET for use across operating systems. xBIM also offers `Xbim.IDS.Validator.Core`, which says it can translate IDS files into executable specifications and run them against **IFC2x3, IFC4, or IFC4.3** models across platforms.

For **data dictionaries and property semantics**, the buildingSMART **bSDD** is important even though it is primarily an API/service rather than a mature .NET package family. buildingSMART describes bSDD as an online service hosting dictionaries with **classifications, properties, allowed values, units, and translations**, and `Xbim.Properties` explicitly exposes **buildingSMART properties, Psets, and related data dictionaries** programmatically. That combination is highly relevant if your materials folder is eventually going to support standards-backed metadata instead of ad hoc labels.

**Ara3D** is the most interesting newer entrant. The Ara 3D IFC Toolkit is described as a set of open-source C# libraries on **.NET 8.0** for loading and querying IFC STEP files, and the older `web-ifc-dotnet` wrapper is explicitly marked **deprecated** because C++/CLI restricted it to Windows. `Ara3D.SDK` presents itself as a cross-platform AEC-oriented SDK compatible with **.NET 8**, and its subprojects include `Ara3D.IO.IfcParser`, `Ara3D.IO.StepParser`, `Ara3D.IO.VIM`, `Ara3D.Geometry`, `Ara3D.Models`, and others. On top of that, `Ara3D.BimOpenSchema` introduces a BIM analytics layer optimized for **Parquet and DuckDB**, which is extremely relevant if you want downstream querying and analysis over your materials and component universe.

**Speckle** is highly relevant as an interoperability and object-exchange layer, though less ideal as your canonical materials kernel. Speckle’s next-generation `.NET` SDK uses **.NET Standard 2.0** and has been **tested on Windows and macOS**. The current ecosystem includes `Speckle.Sdk`, `Speckle.Objects`, and the older `Speckle.Core`, while some IFC-related importer packages such as `Speckle.Importers.Ifc` and `Speckle.WebIfc.Importer` are already marked **deprecated**. In practice, Speckle is strongest as a **connector and exchange hub**, not as the authoritative internal domain model for your monorepo.

### Geometry, sections, analysis, and fabrication-adjacent libraries

For **structural analysis and section mechanics**, **VividOrange** should be your first look, especially `VividOrange.Profiles.Catalogue`, `Sections`, `Sections.SectionProperties`, `IForceMomentInteraction`, `ForceMomentInteraction`, and `InteractionDiagram`. These are recent, structured, and directly aligned with geometry, sections, materials, loads, and interaction diagrams.

For **managed FEA**, `BriefFiniteElement.NET` remains a strong adjunct. It is a fully managed library for **static and linear analysis of solids and structures**, and the current NuGet family includes `BriefFiniteElement.NET`, `BriefFiniteElementNet.Common`, and `BriefFiniteElementNet.CustomElements`, all targeting **.NET 6.0** and/or **.NET Standard 2.0**. There is also a non-linear branch on GitHub, but I would treat that as exploratory rather than production-default.

For **numerics and sparse solving**, the dependable low-level stack is `MathNet.Numerics` plus `CSparse`. Math.NET explicitly states support for **Windows, Linux, and Mac**, while CSparse provides sparse LU, Cholesky, and QR decomposition. Those two are the obvious choices if your composition engine later grows into optimization, stiffness assembly, or section-property derivations that need real numerical infrastructure.

For **units and typed quantities**, `UnitsNet` is essential. Its runtime-extensible quantity system is exactly what you want for a materials library that must safely carry **lengths, areas, densities, stresses, thermal values, and manufacturing dimensions** without unit ambiguity. VividOrange itself already depends on UnitsNet in its force-moment interaction layer.

For **2D computational geometry**, use `NetTopologySuite`, `Clipper2`, and `MIConvexHull`. NTS is the standard .NET topology stack for 2D geometry predicates and manipulations; Clipper2 gives robust polygon clipping, offsetting, and triangulation; MIConvexHull adds convex hulls, Delaunay triangulations, and Voronoi meshes. This matters for masonry bonds, hatch/bond generation, section outlines, perforation logic, nesting, and fast footprint operations.

For **CAD/fabrication interchange**, `IxMilia.Dxf` and `DSTV.Net` are especially interesting. `IxMilia.Dxf` is a portable DXF/DXB reader-writer, and `DSTV.Net` is a steel-fabrication-oriented library that parses DSTV data, exposes profile dimensions such as flange width and web thickness, and can generate SVG from plate geometry. If your steel subsystem eventually flows toward fabrication or CNC, DSTV.Net is a meaningful specialist package.

## Recommended add list

If I were building your monorepo today, I would split the package selection into **adopt now**, **evaluate carefully**, and **do not use as a primary dependency**.

The **adopt now** tier would be: `VividOrange` core packages for domain taxonomy and structural sections; `Hypar.Elements`, `Hypar.IFC4`, and `Hypar.Elements.Serialization.IFC` for code-first BIM and IFC round-tripping; `Ara3D.SDK`, `Ara3D.BimOpenSchema`, and the Ara 3D IFC Toolkit for modern IFC parsing plus analytics; `ids-lib` or `Xbim.IDS.Validator.Core` for IDS validation; `Xbim.Properties` for access to buildingSMART property sets and dictionaries; `UnitsNet`, `MathNet.Numerics`, `CSparse`, `NetTopologySuite`, and `Clipper2` as foundational infrastructure; and `BriefFiniteElement.NET` if you want a managed FEA kernel inside the same monorepo.

The **evaluate carefully** tier would be `xBIM` broader packages, `Speckle`, `BHoM`, and `DSTV.Net`. xBIM is powerful but mixed-platform because some of its most famous geometry/database components are Windows-only; Speckle is excellent for exchange but I would not let it define my canonical materials schema; BHoM has a large and useful conceptual surface but the ecosystem is heavily prerelease and includes legacy .NET Framework/UI components; DSTV.Net is specialized and valuable if steel fabrication is in scope.

The **do not use as a primary dependency** tier is short: `Wosad`, `Xbim.IO.Esent`, `Xbim.Geometry`, and Windows-bound structural wrappers such as `EtabSharp`. They are either Windows-only, .NET Framework-only, or both.

## Monorepo design for your materials domain

The best shape for this repository is a **layered domain model**, not a flat “materials folder.” The first layer should be your **canonical taxonomy**: material families, products, assemblies, connectors, profiles, bonds/patterns, standards references, units, and uncertainty models. VividOrange is currently the closest public package family to that shape because it already separates **materials, profiles, sections, loads, cases, stages, standards, serialization, and uncertainties** into distinct packages.

The second layer should be a **catalog layer** that you own. This is where brick dimensions, coursing families, bond rules, steel catalogs, bolt grades, washer stacks, joist metadata, hanger product lines, and manufacturer SKUs belong. Public packages do not currently give you a mature cross-platform catalog for those product-level details, so this should be modeled as your own data assets with imports from standards bodies, manufacturer exports, or classification dictionaries. The most promising standards/data-dictionary hooks for that layer are bSDD and xBIM property/dictionary tooling.

The third layer should be your **geometry/shape layer**. This is where section outlines, bonds, patterns, cutouts, offsets, plate shapes, coursing layouts, and generated solids live. Pairing `VividOrange.Geometry` and section packages with `NetTopologySuite`, `Clipper2`, and where necessary `MIConvexHull` is a strong solution for 2D-first construction geometry. If you later need richer scene or mesh processing, Ara3D and Hypar give you a credible 3D path.

The fourth layer should be **openBIM interchange and validation**. Use `Hypar.IFC4`, selective `xBIM`, IDS tooling, and bSDD-backed metadata so that your materials and assemblies can be both internally expressive and externally serializable. If you care about downstream analytics more than round-tripping exact authoring-app semantics, Ara3D’s BIM Open Schema is especially compelling because it is designed for **Parquet/DuckDB-style analytics** rather than only document transport.

The fifth layer should be **analysis and decision support**. This is where section properties, interaction diagrams, load combinations, uncertainty propagation, stiffness models, embodied-carbon attributes, thermal attributes, fabrication weight, and procurement metadata live. VividOrange’s sections/interaction packages plus BriefFiniteElementNet, Math.NET, and CSparse are enough to start building this today without taking on a heavyweight proprietary dependency.

## Open questions and limitations

The highest-confidence findings here are about **package families, platform compatibility, and architectural fit**. What is less certain from currently surfaced public evidence is the full NuGet publication status of some **Wosad subprojects** such as `Wosad.Masonry` and `Wosad.ColdFormedSteel`; they clearly exist in the repository tree, but I did not find equally clear current NuGet pages for each one during this pass.

The other major limitation is that public NuGet ecosystems still do **not** appear to provide a mature, off-the-shelf, cross-platform .NET corpus for **manufacturer-grade masonry bonds, fastener catalogs, hangers, joists, nuts/bolts/washer stacks, or construction-product SKUs**. The right response is not to keep hunting for a mythical one-package solution; it is to treat those as **first-party domain data** inside your monorepo and let external packages handle schema, geometry, interoperability, and analysis. This is an inference from the surveyed package landscape.

## Further consideration

One underappreciated move would be to make your internal canonical format **analytics-native from day one**, not merely object-native. Ara3D’s `BimOpenSchema` is notable here because it frames BIM data for **Parquet and DuckDB** workflows. For a materials-heavy monorepo, that could become disproportionately valuable for quantity takeoff, normalization, procurement intelligence, embodied-carbon aggregation, and training data generation for future layout or synthesis systems.

A second, less obvious point is that **IDS and bSDD integration should not be treated as downstream compliance garnish**. If you wire those into the domain layer early, your materials, profiles, assemblies, and generated compositions can be standards-aware at creation time rather than only validated after export. That substantially reduces semantic drift when you later add IFC serialization or analysis engines.

A third point, which many teams miss, is to separate **“shape catalog”** from **“product catalog.”** `VividOrange.Profiles.Catalogue` is a shape/catalogue primitive for standard steel profiles; that is fundamentally different from a product catalog with manufacturer, coating, lead time, bolt set, or connector SKU metadata. If you preserve that distinction in the monorepo, your future composition engine will stay much cleaner, because it can reason separately about **equivalent geometry**, **code identity**, and **procurement identity**.
