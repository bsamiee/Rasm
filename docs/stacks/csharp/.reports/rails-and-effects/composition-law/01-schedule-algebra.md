# Schedule Algebra As Carrier-Polymorphic Composition Aspect

[DURATION_STREAM_ALGEBRA]:
- A schedule is an abstract record whose single abstract member is `Iterable<Duration> Run()` — it is a lazily-realised, potentially-infinite stream of inter-attempt delays, not a loop. Every named constructor produces a stream: `spaced(d)` is `Forever.Map(_ => d)`, `exponential(seed, factor)` is `Forever.Map((_, i) => (double)seed * Pow(factor, i))` indexed by attempt, `linear(seed, factor)` and `fibonacci(seed)` are dedicated stream records, and `recurs(n)` is not a schedule at all but a `ScheduleTransformer` that `Take(n)`s whatever stream it wraps. The retry/repeat machinery never branches on a counter; it consumes the realised iterator until exhaustion.
- The record carries a `Semigroup` instance and three distinct binary combinators with load-bearing, non-interchangeable semantics. Union (`|`) walks both streams to the length of the longer, yielding `Math.Min` of the surviving pair each step — it is the floor-of-the-two/longest-lived combinator. Intersect (`&`) `Zip`s the two and yields `Math.Max`, terminating at the length of the shorter — it is the ceiling-of-the-two/shortest-lived combinator. Combine/`+` (the `Semigroup` op) appends one stream after the other. The canonical retry shape `recurs(5) | exponential(10*ms)` therefore means "exponential spacing, but the count axis from `recurs` truncates the otherwise-infinite stream to five"; switching `|` to `&` changes both the count discipline and the delay arithmetic silently.
- Transformers (`recurs`, `maxDelay`, `maxCumulativeDelay`, `jitter`, `decorrelate`, `resetAfter`, `repeat`, `intersperse`, `NoDelayOnFirst`, `RepeatForever`) are `readonly struct ScheduleTransformer` wrapping `Func<Schedule,Schedule>`. Their `+` is reverse-application composition — `(f + g).Apply(x) == g.Apply(f.Apply(x))` — so transformer chains read left-to-right in pipeline order, not mathematical `∘` order. A bare transformer implicitly converts to a schedule as `Forever | t`, so `recurs(5)` standing alone is "five no-op recurrences," not an error.
- `Duration` is a `readonly struct` over `double` milliseconds with implicit conversions from `double`, `TimeSpan`, and `Time`; `Time` carries `*`/`/`/`+` operators and `Milliseconds`/`Seconds` projections. This is the entire reason `10*ms` and `2 * seconds` flow directly into schedule constructors with no explicit wrapping — the arithmetic happens in `Time`, the implicit operator lands in `Duration`, and the schedule stream is `double`-valued throughout.

[UNLIFT_TRIANGLE_AS_UNIVERSAL_BINDING]:
- The entire scheduling vocabulary — repeat, retry, the while/until predicate variants, fold, fork, timeout, bracket, post, uninterruptible — is declared exactly once as `static virtual` default methods on the `MonadUnliftIO<M>` trait, each a single line of the form `MapIO(ma, io => io.<ScheduleOp>(...))`. There is no per-carrier scheduling code: a carrier earns the whole algebra by supplying one `static abstract K<M, IO<A>> ToIO<A>(K<M,A>)`.
- `MapIO` is the universal bridge: `ToIO(ma).Bind(io => M.LiftIO(f(io)))`. It unlifts the carrier to expose its inner `IO`, applies the IO-level schedule operation, then relifts. Because every scheduling combinator routes through this one method, the schedule semantics are defined purely against `IO<A>` and inherited structurally by every monad and monad-transformer stack on a path to `IO`. The carrier-polymorphic free functions (`repeat<M,A>(Schedule, K<M,A>) where M : MonadUnliftIO<M>` and siblings) are thin forwarders to the corresponding `*IO()` extension, which forwards to `M.<Op>IO`, which resolves to the `MapIO` default.
- The cost is paid at unlift, not at definition: if any monad on the route to the inner `IO` fails to provide `ToIO`, the lift throws a wrapped exceptional at run time rather than failing to compile — the unlift capability is a runtime contract of the stack, not a compile-time one. A carrier-agnostic algorithm bodied against `K<M,A> where M : MonadUnliftIO<M>` thus gains declarative retry/repeat across `Eff`, `Eff<RT>`, and transformer stacks uniformly, but its scheduling correctness depends on the concrete stack honouring `ToIO` end-to-end.

[SCHEDULE_AS_ITERATOR_FOLD]:
- Retry and repeat are not recursion over a guard; they are a fold over the realised duration iterator. The driver materialises `schedule.PrependZero.Run().GetIterator()` and walks it: on each `Cons(head, tail)` it does `yieldFor(head).Bind(_ => attempt.Bind(continue-or-stop))`, and on `Nil` it terminates with the carried result. `PrependZero` injects a zero-delay element at the head so the first attempt runs immediately and the schedule's first delay applies only between attempt one and two — the delay stream is inter-attempt, never pre-first.
- The terminal carried value differs by direction. Repeat threads the last computed `A` and on iterator exhaustion returns `IO.pure(lastValue)` — exhaustion is success. Retry threads the last `Error` (seeded `Errors.None`) and on exhaustion returns `IO.fail(lastError)` — exhaustion is the final failure surfaced to the caller. The predicate variants (`While`/`Until`) compose with the iterator walk, not with the schedule: the schedule bounds *how many and how spaced*, the predicate bounds *whether the observed value or error warrants another step*, and either reaching its limit halts.
- Resource discipline is encoded in which bracket the driver re-enters per step. Repeat re-enters `Bracket()` each iteration — resources acquired inside a repeated computation are released on each successful step, which is exactly why a repeated computation cannot acquire-and-return a live resource. Retry re-enters `BracketFail()` — resources are released only on the failing attempts, so a successful attempt's resources survive and successive retries do not accumulate acquisitions. Wrapping a retrying unit *inside* an outer `BracketFail` scope is therefore a defect: every failed attempt tears down the outer scope's resources, not merely the inner unit's, because the retry driver's own `BracketFail` nests under the outer one.

[FOLD_SCHEDULE_AS_TIMED_AGGREGATION]:
- `Fold(schedule, seed, folder)` reuses the same iterator-walk but threads an accumulator instead of discarding the bound value: it runs the computation once per schedule tick, folds each result into the state, and the schedule governs both cadence and termination. `Schedule.Forever` makes it an unbounded timed aggregator; a bounded schedule makes it a fixed-window sampler. This is the mechanism for "sample this effect on a cadence and reduce" without an explicit timer or mutable accumulator — the schedule *is* the cadence policy as a value.
- The predicate-terminated variants discriminate on which face of the running pair the predicate inspects: distinct overloads route the predicate to the accumulated state `S`, to the latest produced value `A`, or to the `(State, Value)` tuple, all desugaring to one tuple-predicate internal form. `FoldWhile` continues while the predicate holds; `FoldUntil` continues until it holds. The schedule and the predicate are orthogonal stop conditions joined by short-circuit — the first to fire ends the fold — so a `Forever` schedule plus a state predicate yields a converge-until-stable loop whose only bound is the data.

[POLICY_VALUE_TRANSFORMERS]:
- Backoff hardening is expressed as transformer values layered onto a base stream, never as branches inside the retry body. `jitter(factor, seed)` and `jitter(minRandom, maxRandom, seed)` perturb each delay by a randomised amount; the optional seed makes the perturbation deterministic, which is the seam that lets a jittered schedule be unit-tested by pinning the seed. `decorrelate(factor, seed)` perturbs each delay both up and down around its neighbour to reduce cross-instance correlation — its explicit purpose is many parallel clients de-phasing their retry storms, and it deliberately roughly doubles total schedule length as the cost of de-correlation.
- `maxDelay(d)` caps any single returned delay; `maxCumulativeDelay(d)` halts the schedule once the running sum of delays crosses a budget — the difference is per-step ceiling versus total-time deadline. `resetAfter(d)` restarts the stream from its head once cumulative time crosses a threshold, converting a one-shot backoff into a self-resetting steady-state policy. `NoDelayOnFirst` is `s => s.Tail.Prepend(Zero)` — it drops the schedule's first delay and substitutes zero, distinct from `PrependZero` which *adds* a zero in front without dropping anything. These compose by `+` into one named policy value that travels as data, so retry/repeat call sites carry no backoff arithmetic and no flag set re-deriving the curve.
- Cron-like alignment constructors (`fixedInterval`, `windowed`, `secondOfMinute`, `minuteOfHour`, `hourOfDay`, `dayOfWeek`) take an injectable `Func<DateTime>?` current-time source. The injection point is the determinism seam: a fake clock function makes wall-clock-aligned schedules — "every action snaps to the next ten-second window," "fire on the zeroth second of each minute" — fully reproducible without real time passing, and `fixedInterval` specifically coalesces overruns so slow actions do not let re-runs pile up while `windowed` sleeps to the next boundary regardless of action duration.

[COMPOSED_POLICY_SNIPPET]:

```csharp
static readonly Schedule Backoff =
    (Schedule.recurs(6)
   | Schedule.exponential(50 * ms, factor: 2.0)
   | Schedule.maxDelay(2 * seconds)
   | Schedule.decorrelate(factor: 0.2, seed: 7)
   | Schedule.maxCumulativeDelay(30 * seconds))
   & Schedule.spaced(100 * ms);

static K<M, A> Resilient<M, A>(K<M, A> action)
    where M : MonadUnliftIO<M> =>
    retryWhile(Backoff, action, static e => e is Expected)
        .RepeatUntilIO(Schedule.fixedInterval(5 * seconds), static a => a is Settled);
```
