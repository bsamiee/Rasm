# Scope Projections And The Resource Registry

[PROJECTION_INTENT]:
- The child-environment projections are permutations of one constructor over two ownership bits, and each permutation serves exactly one scoping intent: fresh-registry-fresh-source for full isolation, fresh-registry-shared-source for resource isolation under the parent's cancellation axis, shared-registry-fresh-source for a regionally cancellable sub-tree over shared resources. The dominant scoping bug is projection mismatch — wrapping a sub-tree in the resource-isolated projection when the intent was independent cancellation leaves it bound to the parent token, and cancelling it tears the parent.
- Ownership and disposal are one predicate: an environment disposes exactly the halves it allocated, so a child sharing its parent's source can be disposed without tearing the parent's still-live half. Cancellation cascades one direction only — the inherited token is registered to cancel the child source, never the reverse — which is why the cancel-isolated projection is the safe unit for speculative or deadline-bounded work whose abort must not reach the enclosing computation. A deadline is structurally the same projection plus an expiring source, so a timed region nests with an inner regional-cancel region as two child sources, the inner strictly shorter-lived.

[REGISTRY_IDENTITY_AND_ORDER]:
- The registry keys tracked resources by bare reference identity over a closed family of release cases — sync-disposable, async-disposable, and free-function. Two structurally-equal but distinct instances are two independent entries, and a value-type resource boxes to a fresh key on every acquire, so its release fires once per acquire call rather than once per logical value.
- Bulk teardown enumerates the registry's trie in hash-derived order: there is no last-in-first-out and no insertion-order guarantee. Teardown that must release a dependent before its dependency encodes the ordering inside a single composite resource's release arrow — relying on two separate registrations imports a stack-unwind mental model the registry does not provide, and this is the sharpest correctness trap on the whole resource surface.
- Acquisition never consults the parent scope; only release-on-miss delegates upward. A child's registry is a strict local set — the parent's entries are invisible to the child's bulk teardown, while an outer-acquired handle remains explicitly releasable from inside through the upward walk.
- Every release arrow runs on a freshly-built environment carrying a non-cancellable token, so cleanup always runs to completion under cancellation — and a release arrow that itself observes a token sees the non-cancellable one and cannot be cut short. Long token-aware work does not belong in a disposer.

[EAGER_ACQUIRE_DEFERRED_RELEASE]:
- A scoped acquire registers the instant the acquire effect resolves; the release sits in the registry until the nearest enclosing auto-freeing scope disposes. A bare scoped acquire with no surrounding bracket therefore holds its resource until the outermost run completes — resource pressure under a long-running root effect is almost always many eager acquires with no intervening scope to bound their release.
- The unconditional and fail-only scopes differ only on the success path — one frees unconditionally, the other leaves the success path's resource subtree alive — and that divergence is the entire escape mechanism by which an acquiring computation hands a live handle to its caller's scope.

[FORK_SUBTREE_AND_RACE_TEARDOWN]:
- A fork projects the scope lattice onto a pool thread: a private source cancelled by the parent token, and a child tracker parented to the forking scope's tracker. Pre-fork handles are reachable and releasable from inside the fork through the upward walk, while anything acquired inside is reclaimed by the child and never leaks upward. The fork handle is a pure value pair of cancel and await effects, so awaiting can happen in a different scope after the spawning region unwinds — the fork's lifetime is bounded by its private source, not by the spawner's stack frame.
- Race teardown is asymmetric across the two race ingress shapes, and the asymmetry decides loser cleanup. Racing raw effects runs every competitor under one shared cancel-isolated child environment and cancels that source the moment the winner lands — losers are torn down deterministically. Racing already-running fork handles awaits them under the caller's environment and cancels only its own local await scope: the losing forks' private sources are untouched and they keep running to their own completion. Deterministic teardown of racing forks requires explicitly cancelling each loser through its own cancel handle after the race resolves.

[HOST_THREAD_SEAM]:
- Posting onto a captured synchronization context is a scope operation: with no captured context the post degrades to a direct run; with one, the body is marshalled through the context while the poster awaits an auto-reset signal under the ambient token — cancellation releases the waiter and surfaces as cancellation rather than dangling, and a marshalled-body exception is rethrown after the wait completes. The captured context rides the environment and is forked or re-captured by the scope projections, so "which thread runs this continuation or cleanup" is answered by the environment, never by an implicit capture at the call site.
